What is Quantum Computing?

Quantum computing is a form of computation that makes use of quantum mechanics phenomena to effectively increase the efficiency of computation exponentially.

Quantum Computers are essentially a quantum counterpart to classical computeres.

Although there may not be any extremely complex calculations that can be solved any faster in quantum computers than in classical computers, there will be one day, and when that day comes we will be ready.

When and where was the concept first applied?

First proposed in the 1980s by Richard Feynman and Yuri Manin, quantum computing was first applied in 1998 by three researchers Neil Gershenfeld, Isaac Chuang, and Mark Kubinec. Each from MIT, the Los Alamos National Laboratory, and UC Berkley respectively.

How does it work?

Classical computers work through billions of transistors which can only store data in binary form, that is either a 0 or a 1.

Quantum computers work with qubits which can essentially model a quantum phenomena called superposition, meaning they can be anything from 0 to 1 and even a mixture of anything from 0 to 1(inclusive)

Qubits alone can't do much, but with a ton of qubits certain quantum algorithms can be used to exploit another quantum phenomena called entanglement, which allows for each qubit to be individual yet continually linked.
